# Types of Bank Accounts

## Checking Accounts

A checking account is a transactional bank account that allows customers to make deposits and withdrawals, as well as conduct various payments and transactions. These accounts typically come with a debit card and a checkbook, enabling customers to access their funds easily for everyday expenses and purchases. Checking accounts also offer online and mobile banking access, allowing customers to monitor their balances, transfer funds, and pay bills conveniently from their devices. The primary purpose of a checking account is to manage day-to-day cash flow and facilitate financial transactions.

## Savings Accounts

Savings accounts are interest-bearing bank accounts designed for holding and growing one's money over time. Unlike checking accounts, savings accounts generally offer higher interest rates, which means that the money deposited into these accounts will earn a return based on the prevailing interest rate. However, savings accounts often have limitations on the number of withdrawals or transfers that can be made per month. The main purpose of a savings account is to provide a secure place to build an emergency fund, save for specific financial goals, such as a down payment on a house or a vacation, and earn interest on the deposited funds.

## Money Market Accounts

A money market account is a type of savings account that typically offers higher interest rates than regular savings accounts. These accounts combine features of both checking and savings accounts, allowing customers to earn higher yields while still maintaining some liquidity. Money market accounts usually come with limitations on the number of transactions that can be made per month, but they often provide check-writing privileges and debit card access. The primary purpose of a money market account is to earn a higher rate of return on funds that are not needed immediately while still having some flexibility to access the money when needed.

## Certificates of Deposit (CDs)

Certificates of Deposit (CDs) are time deposits that pay a fixed interest rate over a specified period, ranging from a few months to several years. When opening a CD, customers agree to leave their money deposited for the entire term, and in exchange, they receive a guaranteed interest rate that is typically higher than regular savings accounts. However, if the customer wishes to withdraw the funds before the CD matures, they may be subject to early withdrawal penalties. The main purpose of CDs is to earn a guaranteed return on investment for a fixed term, making them a suitable option for investors who prioritize stability and predictability over liquidity.
